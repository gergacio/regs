Skilled person (electrically)

Person who possesses, as appropriate to the nature of the electrical work to be undertaken adequate education, training and practical skills, and who is able to perceive risks and avoid hazards which electricity can create.

-

Extraneous-conductive-part

A conductive part liable to introduce a potential, generally Earth potential, and not forming part of the electrical installation.

Section 410 - Introduction

The fundamental rule of protection against electric shock:

hazardous-live-parts shall not be accessible and accessible conductive parts shall not be hazardous- live, both under normal conditions and under single fault conditions

-

Regulation 410.3.2

A protective measure shall consist of: (i) an appropriate combination of a provision for basic protection and an independent provision for fault protection, or (ii) an enhanced protective provision which provides both basic protection and fault protection.

-

Regulation 410.3.3

The following protective measures generally are permitted: (i) automatic disconnection of supply (Section 411) (ii) double or reinforced insulation (Section 412) (iii) electrical separation for the supply to one item of current-using equipment (Section 413) (iv) extra-low voltage (SELV and PELV) (Section 414)

Section 411 - Automatic Disconnection of Supply (ADS)

This measure of protection is the most commonly used protective measure.

-

Regulation 411.1 General

Automatic disconnection of supply is a protective measure in which: (i) basic protection is provided by basic insulation of live parts or by barriers or enclosures, in accordance with Section 416,

(ii) fault protection is provided by protective earthing, protective equipotential bonding and automatic disconnection in case of a fault, in accordance with Regulations 411.3 to 411.6

-

Regulation 411.3.1.1 - Protective earthing

Exposed-conductive-parts shall be connected to a protective conductor under the specific conditions for each type of system earthing A circuit protective conductor shall be run to and terminated at each point in wiring and at each accessory except a lampholder having no exposed-conductive-parts.

-

Regulation 411.3.1.2 - Protective Equipotential Bonding

Extraneous-conductive-part. A conductive part liable to introduce a potential, generally Earth potential, and not forming part of the electrical installation.

-

Regulation 411.3.2 - Automatic disconnection in case of a fault

Protective devices must interrupt the supply to the line conductor of a circuit or equipment in the event of a fault of negligible impedance between the line conductor and an exposed-conductive-part within the disconnection time required by Regulations 411.3.2.2, 411.3.2.3 and 411.3.2.4.

Section 412 - Double or Reinforced Insulation

Regulation 412.1.1

Double or reinforced insulation is a protective measure in which: (i) basic protection is provided by basic insulation and fault protection is provided by supplementary insulation,

(ii) basic and fault protection is provided by reinforced insulation between live parts and accessible parts.

NOTE: This protective measure is intended to prevent the appearance of a dangerous voltage on the accessible parts of electrical equipment through a fault in the basic insulation.

-

Class I equipment.

Equipment in which protection against electric shock does not rely on basic insulation only, but which includes means for the connection of exposed-conductive-parts to a protective conductor in the fixed wiring of the installation.

Class II equipment

'Equipment in which protection against electric shock does not rely on basic insulation only, but in which additional safety precautions such as supplementary insulation are provided, there being no provision for the connection of exposed metalwork of the equipment to a protective conductor.'

-

Regulation 412.2.3.2

Circuit supplying one or more items of Class II equipment shall have a circuit protective conductor run to and terminated at each point in wiring and at each accessory.

Section 413 - Electrical Separation

-

Regulation 413.1.1

Electrical separation is a protective measure in which:

(i) basic protection is provided by basic insulation of live parts or by barriers or enclosures in accordance with Section 416, and

(ii) fault protection is provided by simple separation of the separated circuit from other circuits and from Earth.

Section 414 - Extra-low voltage provided by SELV/PELV

-

Regulation 414.2

Both basic protection and fault protection are deemed to be provided where:

(i) the nominal voltage cannot exceed the upper limit of voltage Band I, and

(ii) the supply is from one of the sources listed in Regulation 414.3, and

(iii) the conditions of Regulation 414.4 are fulfilled.

Definitions of voltage bands are as follows:

(a) Band I circuit: a circuit that is nominally extra-low voltage, i.e. not exceeding 50 V AC or 120 V DC: for example, SELV, PELV, information and communication, data and signalling; and

(b) Band II circuit: a circuit that is nominally low voltage, i.e. 51 to 1000 V AC and 121 to 1500 V DC.

SELV system

In this protective measure, the voltage is 'stepped down' from 230 V to 50 V AC or less (120 V or less DC). The secondary windings on the transformer have less turns than on the primary, enabling the voltage to be lowered to the desired level.

As with electrical separation, there is no connection to earth on the secondary side so in the event of a fault, the shock hazard is greatly reduced. SELV equipment can be classified as CLASS III equipment.

PELV system

An extra-low voltage system which is not electrically separated from Earth, but which otherwise satisfies all the requirements for SELV.

The voltage is extra-low but both the primary input and secondary output have a connection to earth.

In the event of a fault, the touch voltage is limited to a maximum of 50 V AC or 120 V DC.

-

Regulation 414.3 Sources for SELV and PELV

(i) BS EN 61558-2-6 Safety Isolating Transformer

An example of a SELV transformer - connection to earth on the primary side but no connection to earth on the secondary (output) side.

The symbol shown will often be seen on SELV equipment and is the symbol for Class III equipment.

(ii) Motor-generator set meeting the requirements for a SELV source

(iii) Batteries or engine-driven generators meeting the requirements for a SELV source

-

Regulation 414.4 Requirements for SELV and PELV circuits

(i) basic insulation between live parts and other SELV or PELV circuits, and

(ii) protective separation from live parts of circuits not being SELV or PELV, provided by double or reinforced insulation or by basic insulation and protective screening for the highest voltage present.

SELV circuits shall have basic insulation between live parts and Earth.

The PELV circuits and/or exposed-conductive-parts of equipment supplied by the PELV circuits may be earthed.

Section 416 - Provisions for Basic Protection

Basic protection can be provided by insulation of live parts and/or barriers or enclosures or by obstacles and placing out of reach (which we'll look at in Section 417 following this lesson).

-

Regulation 416.1 - Basic insulation of live parts

Live parts shall be completely covered with insulation which can only be removed by destruction.

NOTE: The insulation is intended to prevent contact with live parts.

-

Regulation 416.2 - Barriers or enclosures

NOTE: Barriers or enclosures are intended to prevent contact with live parts.

Regulation 416.2.1

Live parts shall be inside enclosures or behind barriers providing at least the degree of protection IPXXB or IP2X. Let's look at IP codes

IP Codes

IP has been defined in the past as 'Ingress Protection', then 'Index of Protection' and now in BS 7671 as 'International Protection'.

An IP Code details an enclosure's resistance to solids (dust/dirt) and liquids (water), using two digits: the first (0-6) for solids (e.g., 6 is dust-tight), and the second (0-9) for liquids (e.g., 8 is continuous immersion).

Common examples include IP65 (dust-tight, protected against water jets) or IP44 (protected from 1 mm solids and splashes).

An IP code with an 'X' indicates 'Not Applicable'. For example, IP4X specifies that protection against the 1.0 mm probe is required and that there is no requirement for the protection from the ingress of water.

IPXXB

IPXXB is an IP code used to specify how well an electrical enclosure prevents people from accessing hazardous live parts.

IP = International Protection X = No rating specified for solid object protection X = No rating specified for water protection B = Additional letter indicating protection against access with a finger

So IPXXB specifically describes protection against accidental contact with live parts using a standard test finger. The equipment must prevent a jointed test finger (12 mm diameter, 80 mm long) from touching dangerous internal parts.

IPXXD

IPXXD is an IP code where: IP = International Protection XX = No rating for solid‑object ingress or water ingress D = Additional letter indicating protection against access with a test wire

This means the enclosure is tested to ensure that a wire of 1.0 mm diameter and 100 mm length cannot reach live hazardous parts.

Section 417 - Obstacles and Placing Out of Reach

The protective measures of obstacles and placing out of reach provide basic protection only.

They are for application in installations, with or without fault protection, that are controlled or supervised by skilled persons.

-

417.2 Obstacles

NOTE: Obstacles are intended to prevent unintentional contact with live parts but not intentional contact by deliberate circumvention of the obstacle.

-

Regulation 417.2.1 Obstacles

(i) unintentional bodily approach to live parts, and (ii) unintentional contact with live parts during the operation of live equipment in normal service.

-

Regulation 417.2.2 Obstacles

An obstacle may be removed without the use of a key or tool but shall be secured so as to prevent unintentional removal.

-

Regulation 417.3 Placing out of Reach

NOTE: Protection by placing out of reach is intended only to prevent unintentional contact with live parts.

A bare or insulated overhead line for distribution between buildings and structures shall be installed to the standard required by the Electricity Safety, Quality and Continuity Regulations.

Sections 418 and 419

Protective Measures for Application/Provisions where ADS is not Feasible

-

418.1 Non-conducting location

A non‑conducting location provides both basic and fault protection without relying on protective earthing. It is therefore included in BS 7671 Section 418 as a recognised (but restrictive) shock‑protection method.

It is only suitable in locations supervised by skilled or instructed persons, and only when all the required conditions can be controlled.

-

418.2 Protection by earth-free local equipotential bonding

Protection by earth‑free local equipotential bonding is a shock‑protection measure described in BS 7671 (Section 418) where all exposed‑conductive‑parts inside a location are bonded to each other but not connected to Earth.

This method of protection shall be used only in special circumstances.

-

418.3 Electrical separation for the supply to more than one item of current using equipment

According to BS 7671, electrical separation for the supply to more than one item of equipment is NOT generally permitted, because it becomes unsafe unless very strict conditions are applied.

BS 7671 Section 704 (construction sites) explicitly states that “electrical separation for the supply of more than one item of current‑using equipment (418.3) shall not be used.”

This reflects a wider safety principle: If multiple devices share the same separated source, exposed‑conductive‑parts on different items could reach different fault voltages. A person touching two simultaneously could complete a shock path between them even though the system is unearthed.

Section 419

419.1 Where automatic disconnection is not feasible in circumstances where:

(i) electronic equipment with limited short-circuit current is installed, or

(ii) the required disconnection times cannot be achieved by a protective device

the provisions of Regulations 419.2 and 419.3 are applicable.
